Ultrasound Imaging Techniques for Bone Surface Segmentation

Summary
Ultrasound imaging has emerged as a promising non-ionising modality for visualising bone surfaces, offering real-time feedback, portability and cost efficiency compared with conventional CT or MRI. Bone–soft-tissue interfaces produce strong echoes, yet the interpretation of these echoes is hampered by speckle noise, acoustic shadowing and limited field-of-view. Traditional approaches to bone segmentation have relied on heuristic filters, edge detectors and active shape models to delineate cortical margins in two-dimensional B-mode images. More recently, freehand three-dimensional ultrasound acquisition combined with image registration to preoperative volumes has enabled volumetric reconstruction of bone structures intraoperatively. The past several years have witnessed a shift towards machine-learning frameworks—principally convolutional neural networks—for automated bone contour extraction, followed by statistical shape-model-based completion of occluded regions. These advances are driving practical applications in computer-assisted orthopaedic surgery, arthroplasty planning and ultrasound-guided interventions, with submillimetric accuracy now achievable in live settings.

Research from all publishers
A lightweight neural network framework has been developed to achieve accurate, real-time segmentation of bone surfaces in two-dimensional ultrasound. By adopting a U-Net backbone, incorporating a dual attention mechanism to capture spatial and channel correlations, and employing a level-set-informed loss function, this approach attains a Dice similarity coefficient approaching 89% at 21 frames per second. Its streamlined architecture holds promise for ultrasound-guided orthopaedic navigation, where rapid feedback is essential.

A comprehensive review of ultrasound-based three-dimensional bone modelling highlights the predominance of machine-learning methods for segmentation but notes a gap in the application of learning-based model completion techniques. The survey examines algorithms for extracting cortical surfaces, outlines the challenges of limited training data and standardised metrics, and forecasts the need for open datasets to enhance generalisability. Identified trends include hybrid pipelines that couple neural segmenters with model-driven reconstruction to overcome occlusions and poor signal regions.

In knee arthroplasty planning, a pipeline combining freehand 3D ultrasound acquisition with a convolutional neural network and statistical shape model has been shown to reconstruct femoral and tibial bone surfaces within submillimetre error margins relative to MRI references. Partial volumetric scans are segmented in real time, then completed via a pre-learnt shape prior, yielding full bone models in under one minute of processing. This proof-of-concept demonstrates the feasibility of ultrasound-only workflows for patient-specific implant design and intraoperative guidance.

Technical terms
Speckle noise: Grainy interference pattern in ultrasound caused by coherent scattering, which obscures fine structural details.

U-Net: A convolutional neural network architecture with symmetric encoder–decoder paths and skip connections, widely used for biomedical image segmentation.

Statistical Shape Model (SSM): A deformable model constructed from a training set of anatomies, used to infer complete structures from partial observations.

Dice similarity coefficient: A metric for overlap between predicted and reference segmentations, ranging from 0 (no overlap) to 1 (perfect match).

Level-set loss: A segmentation loss function that emphasises boundary localisation by modelling contour evolution in the network’s output.
